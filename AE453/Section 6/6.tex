\section{Intro}
We can specify the state of a plasma via the species continuity equation:
%equillibirum ionization which will lead to SAHA equation. 
\begin{shaded}
\textbf{State of Plasma}
 \begin{equation}
 \begin{aligned}
 \frac{\partial n_i}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (n_i \, \vv{v_i}) = \sum_j \omega_{ij}
 \end{aligned}
 \end{equation}
Where
 \begin{equation*}
 \begin{aligned}
  \frac{\partial n_i}{\partial t} & \text{ is the rate of change of species i in the volume} \\ \\
 n_i \, \vv{v_i} & \text{ is the convection rate for species i in or out of the volume} \\ \\
 \omega_{ij} & \text{ is the net generation of species i  in the volume due to process j}
 \end{aligned}
 \end{equation*}
 
 Previously, we had seen continuity as current but now we are considering species continuity. Note: the continuity is not equal to zero because we have species creation and destruction within the volume.
 \end{shaded}
 For instance, we could use
 \vspace{-5mm}
 \begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Electron-impact ionization:} 
 \vspace{-1mm}
 \begin{itemize}
 \item The rate at which species are being created via ionization {\Large$\omega_{i1}=k_1n_en_i$}
 \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Radiative recombination:} 
 \vspace{-1mm}
 \begin{itemize}
 \item The rate at which species are being removed via recombination. {\Large$\omega_{i2}=k_2n_i$}
 \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
And so on through all the processes seen in Chapter 5.
 
We could, in principle solve N equations for N species if we knew all the reaction rate constants     $k_j$        	.   For very simple, low-density plasmas, we use the \underline{corona model} to do just that.   For higher-density plasmas we have to use a \underline{collisional-radiative model}, which is more general but requires lots of number-crunching and assumptions.
 
For really high density, the gas reaches equilibrium, and ionization modeling becomes much simpler and we use an \underline{equilibrium model}. This model of equilibrium ionization will lead us to deriving the SAHA equation.
